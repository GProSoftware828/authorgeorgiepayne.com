Topic 4: Product Positioning 4/28/2018

After this lengthy hiatus from blogging it is now time to roll the red carpet out on our next new topic: where in the marketplace does a given product associate itself? Of all the various approaches to this concern, the first step, on the kernel level of going from taking inventory of possibilities to sensing enough opportunity to develop a motivation, is to determine who the consumers/fanbase/customers are.

This first step is possibly the most cerebral, but might also be the most scientific. Not all judgment on this nascent positioning is empirical, unless the product conceiver has personally spoken with each [some number] potential fan individually and knows how they would feel about his product... Which leads us to our final prim and proper assessment.

There is an elaboration on the audience-based product conception that encompasses socially-defining the product as it becomes a marketplace participant called 'social proof'. The first social proof a product has is always the rationality of its creator. Then the needed social proof that as humans our hard wiring requires in order to put our trust into a product often comes from the institutional filters of a distribution social system, but equally as often it comes from individual connections with the public within the product's target market crowd in a plotted web.

Topic 2: Character Development 11/30/2017

There is a sort of binary approach to character description in the narrative school of thought. One of my favorite authors develops a character with interactions and situations, and I can read for 80,000 words and not know the hair color or outfit of the main character. This builds upon the reader's personal background to project their sense of character and creates a sort of visceral reading experience, however, it requires occassional ostensible scenes not related to the storyline to develop the character if we are not being told what style his button shirt is, or what fit her jeans are, etc. For example, one book I am reading has a main character that is a lawyer, so it is implied that he is usually in some sort of broken down formal outfit during his travels.

The other side of the coin is that description is used indirectly to move the story along, while using possibly somewhat demonstrative scenes to build the character superceding the storyline. For instance, a recent book I read described an archeologist's outfit as 'chic' to reinforce the fact that the characters were under the donminion of a foreign environment. I use descriptions of outfits as either a supplement or a locum for a sex scene when the privacy hasn't yet been established.

If I Know HTML and CSS, do I know HTML5 and CSS3?

I would say if you know one you know the other. It's not like Java and Javascript where they are completely different languages with similar names. It's more like C++ where the ISO/EIC updates its standard library every three years: 2011, 2014, 2017, etc.

It's the same language with a few new formats from the original. The trick is, some people reading your resume don't know this and will assume you only know a language that is thirty years old.
